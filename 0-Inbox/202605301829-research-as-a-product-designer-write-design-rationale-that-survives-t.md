# Research: As a Product Designer, write design rationale that survives the review meeting

**Design Rationale – How to Make It Survive the Review Meeting**

| Element | Why It Matters (Evidence‑Based) | How We Show It |
|---------|--------------------------------|----------------|
| **Clear problem statement** | A product exists to *fulfil a customer need* and deliver *utility* [1]. Starting with the core benefit (the “why”) frames every decision and prevents scope creep. | One‑sentence problem + user quote; link to research data. |
| **User‑centered goals** | The *expected product* is what the customer assumes will be delivered; missing any of those expectations leads to disappointment [1]. | List measurable success criteria (e.g., “reduce task‑completion time by 20 %”). |
| **Evidence‑backed assumptions** | Effective products are built on *thorough market research* and *innovation* [1]. Reviewers will challenge any claim that isn’t sourced. | Cite user interviews, analytics, competitive audit. |
| **Design decisions tied to levels of the product** | Kotler’s five levels (core, basic, expected, augmented, potential) give a hierarchy for justification [1]. Showing how each decision moves the design from “basic” to “augmented” demonstrates added value. | • Core: solves the need.<br>• Basic: functional UI.<br>• Expected: accessibility, performance.<br>• Augmented: micro‑interactions, personalization.<br>• Potential: future extensions. |
| **Trade‑off analysis** | Every product must balance *cost* and *lifespan*; reviewers look for transparent trade‑offs rather than “nice‑to‑have” features [1]. | Table of alternatives, impact vs. effort, ROI estimate. |
| **Metrics & success measurement** | The *augmented product* is validated by post‑launch metrics (adoption, satisfaction, revenue) [1]. Quantifiable targets give reviewers confidence the design is not speculative. | Dashboard mock‑up with KPIs (NPS, conversion, churn). |
| **Risk mitigation** | Highlighting *potential product* risks (technical debt, regulatory, scalability) shows foresight and aligns with the “commitment to quality” principle [1]. | Risk register with probability, impact, mitigation plan. |
| **Storytelling flow** | A concise narrative—problem → research → solution hierarchy → trade‑offs → metrics—mirrors the *customer‑centric* view of a product and keeps reviewers focused [1]. | 5‑slide deck: 1) Problem, 2) Research, 3) Design hierarchy, 4) Trade‑offs, 5) Success metrics. |

### Quick Checklist for the Review

1. **Start with the core benefit** – “What need are we solving?”  
2. **Show user evidence** – quotes, data, personas.  
3. **Map each design choice to a product level** (core → potential).  
4. **Present a transparent cost/benefit matrix**.  
5. **Define measurable success criteria** and a post‑launch measurement plan.  
6. **List top‑3 risks and mitigation** – demonstrate readiness.  

By grounding every claim in the product‑definition framework (utility, expected features, augmented value) and coupling it with concrete data and metrics, the rationale becomes a defensible, decision‑ready artifact that reviewers can quickly validate rather than debate.
