# Multi-perspective research: these vendor quotes for an annual seat license and recommend one: Vendor A — $48/seat/yr, 100-seat min, includes SAML SSO + audit log. Vendor B — $39/seat/yr, 50-seat min, SAML SSO is +$8/seat add-on,

## Topic statement  
An organization must choose between two SaaS vendors that quote annual seat‑based licenses: Vendor A charges **$48 per seat per year** with a **100‑seat minimum** and includes SAML SSO + audit logging; Vendor B charges **$39 per seat per year** with a **50‑seat minimum** but SAML SSO is an **add‑on at $8 per seat**. The question is which vendor offers the better overall value when the required features (SSO and audit log) and likely seat count are taken into account.

## Perspectives  

### mainstream  
The mainstream view of seat‑based pricing defines a “seat” as a named user and notes that minimum seat commitments affect total cost [2]. It also points out that many SaaS vendors treat SSO as a premium feature that can increase the price by more than 10 % over the base rate [4][8].  

### critical  
The “SSO tax” literature explicitly calls out the practice of charging extra for SSO, describing it as a common up‑charge that makes security features prohibitively expensive for smaller organizations [4][8]. No specific criticism of the two vendors is provided, but the source frames any separate SSO fee as a negative pricing practice.  

### practitioner  
Practitioner‑focused sources on licensing (e.g., Microsoft licensing guides) explain that total cost of ownership must include required add‑ons such as audit logging or SSO, and that minimum seat thresholds can force organizations to purchase more seats than they need [3][5]. No direct comparison of Vendor A vs. B is given.  

### recent  
Recent “SSO tax” listings again show that SSO add‑ons typically raise the per‑seat price by roughly 20 %–30 % (e.g., $4 → $8 per user/month for 1Password) [4][8]. No new data on the two specific vendors is available.  

## Cross‑cutting themes  
- **SSO is frequently an add‑on that raises per‑seat cost** (≈ 20 %–30 % increase) [4][8].  
- **Seat minimums affect total spend**; a higher minimum forces purchase of unused seats [2][5].  
- **Audit logging is often bundled with higher‑tier plans**, so its inclusion in Vendor A’s base price may be a differentiator [3].  

## Open questions  
- **Actual seat count needed**: If the organization needs fewer than 100 seats, Vendor A’s minimum could make it more expensive; the exact break‑even point is not calculated in the sources.  
- **Cost of audit logging for Vendor B**: No source specifies whether Vendor B charges extra for audit logs, leaving a potential hidden cost.  
- **Feature parity beyond SSO and audit log**: The sources do not compare other functional differences (performance, support, integration) that could sway the decision.  

## Bottom line  
Based on the available evidence, Vendor B offers a lower base price per seat ($39 vs. $48) and a lower minimum commitment (50 vs. 100 seats), but its SAML SSO incurs an $8 add‑on per seat—exactly the “SSO tax” identified as a common industry up‑charge [4][8]. If the organization requires SSO for **all** seats, Vendor B’s effective price becomes $47 per seat, only $1 less than Vendor A’s $48, while still allowing a smaller minimum purchase. However, if audit logging is also required and Vendor B charges separately for it, Vendor A’s bundled offering may be more cost‑effective. The decisive factor is the **actual number of seats needed** and whether any additional fees (audit log, support tiers) apply to Vendor B. In the absence of hidden costs, Vendor B is marginally cheaper and more flexible; if audit logging is essential and not free with Vendor B, Vendor A may provide better overall value.
