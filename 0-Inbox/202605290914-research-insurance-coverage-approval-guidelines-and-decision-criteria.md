# Research: insurance coverage approval guidelines and decision criteria

**Insurance‑coverage approval – typical guidelines & decision criteria**

| Step | What the insurer looks at | Why it matters (decision rule) |
|------|---------------------------|--------------------------------|
| **1. Verify eligibility & compliance** | • Policy‑type requirements (e.g., health, auto, liability) <br>• State‑mandated coverage minimums <br>• NAIC‑set standards and regulator‑issued complaint indexes | Coverage cannot be approved unless the applicant meets the statutory minimums and the insurer’s own compliance checklist. The NAIC compiles state‑level complaints that insurers use to flag high‑risk carriers or products [1]. |
| **2. Underwrite the risk** | • Applicant’s risk profile (age, gender, location, claims history, credit score, health status, property value, etc.) <br>• Specific risk factors for the line of business (e.g., driving record for auto, medical history for health) | Underwriting determines the **premium** and whether the risk is acceptable. Higher perceived risk → higher premium or denial [1]. |
| **3. Set policy limits** | • Maximum amount the insurer will pay per loss, per period, or lifetime <br>• Limits required by law or by the applicant (e.g., ACA‑compliant health plans cannot have lifetime limits on essential benefits) | Limits must be sufficient to cover likely losses but also stay within the insurer’s risk appetite. Higher limits increase premium and may trigger additional underwriting scrutiny [1][3]. |
| **4. Choose deductible / out‑of‑pocket amount** | • Amount the insured will pay before the insurer responds (per claim or per policy) <br>• Relationship between deductible size and premium cost | Deductibles act as a claim‑frequency filter; higher deductibles lower premiums and discourage small, frequent claims [1]. |
| **5. Review exclusions & conditions** | • Specific perils or situations the policy does **not** cover (e.g., certain natural disasters, intentional acts) <br>• Policy‑holder obligations (notice of loss, proof of loss, cooperation with adjuster) | Exclusions define the boundaries of coverage. If a requested loss falls under an exclusion, approval is denied or limited. |
| **6. Final approval & issuance** | • All underwriting, limit, deductible, and compliance checks passed <br>• Premium payment received (or financing arranged) | Only after the above criteria are satisfied does the insurer issue the contract and the coverage becomes effective. |

### Core Decision Criteria (summarized)

1. **Eligibility / Regulatory Fit** – Must satisfy mandatory coverage rules and NAIC‑derived standards.  
2. **Risk Assessment (Underwriting)** – Premium is set based on actuarial evaluation of the applicant’s risk factors.  
3. **Policy Limits** – Must align with both legal minimums and the insurer’s exposure tolerance.  
4. **Deductible Level** – Balances cost to the insured with the insurer’s desire to limit small‑claim volume.  
5. **Exclusions & Conditions** – Define what is *not* covered; any loss matching an exclusion is automatically denied.  
6. **Payment Confirmation** – Premium must be paid (or arranged) before coverage becomes active.

These guidelines are common across most lines of insurance—health, auto, homeowners, business, and life—because they stem from the fundamental components of any policy: **premium, deductible, and policy limit** [1] and the broader underwriting and regulatory framework described in insurance reference works [2][3].
