# Research: List the 5 clauses you check first in a SaaS MSA and describe what a red flag looks like in each one.

**The first five clauses you should read – and the “red‑flag” tell‑tale signs that the clause is risky)**  

| # | Clause you check first | What a red‑flag looks like |
|---|------------------------|----------------------------|
| 1 | **Basic Commercial Terms** (term length, renewal, price, payment schedule, description of the SaaS solution) | • Provider can raise the price un‑ilaterally (no caps or notice period).<br>• Fees are vague or lumped together (e.g., “implementation fees” not broken out, or no clear start‑date for each fee type).<br>• The solution description is generic – it does not list the specific features or functionality you negotiated. |
| 2 | **Termination Rights** | • No “standard” termination triggers (uncured material breach, bankruptcy, etc.).<br>• Provider retains a termination‑for‑convenience right while the customer does not, or the customer‑only convenience right is delayed until after the provider has recouped all implementation costs (often > 2 years).<br>• No termination right tied to a failed acceptance test or post‑implementation rejection. |
| 3 | **Data (Privacy & Security)** | • Provider is not required to comply with applicable data‑privacy laws (GDPR, CCPA, etc.).<br>• No explicit security standards or breach‑notification obligations.<br>• The agreement gives the provider a broad, perpetual license to use the customer’s data beyond “internal use during the term.” |
| 4 | **Service‑Level Agreements (SLAs)** | • No performance metrics (availability %, response‑time, resolution‑time).<br>• Remedies are limited to “sole and exclusive” invoice credits, eliminating the customer’s right to terminate or claim damages.<br>• Maintenance windows are unlimited or undefined, leaving the service at the provider’s discretion. |
| 5 | **Intellectual Property** | • The contract contains an assignment of IP from the customer to the provider (or vice‑versa) without a clear work‑for‑hire clause when custom code is created.<br>• Licenses granted by the customer (e.g., to use its data or logo) are overly broad (“any purpose, worldwide, perpetual”).<br>• No back‑up‑license or guarantee of ownership of any deliverables the customer expects to own. |

**Why these five first?**  
A “major‑red‑flags‑only” review (the common quick‑screen approach for low‑risk SaaS deals) focuses on the same five areas identified by Koley Jessen’s SaaS‑agreement guide [2]. They capture the most common existential and financial risks—price creep, being locked into a bad service, data‑breach exposure, lack of enforceable performance standards, and loss of IP rights. Spotting the red‑flag patterns above lets you decide whether a deeper legal dive is needed before you sign.
