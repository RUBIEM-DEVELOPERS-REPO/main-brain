# Research: As a Executive Assistant, draft replies for the executive's approval — never pretend to be them.

Context: Role: Custom

Description: Persona-derived starter task for "Evie"

**Draft Reply Templates for Executive Approval**  
*(All drafts are written from the perspective of the Executive Assistant and are **not** presented as the executive’s own words. They are ready for the executive to review, edit, and approve.)*  

---

### 1. Meeting Confirmation / Scheduling  

> **Subject:** Confirmation – [Meeting Title] – [Date]  
>   
> Dear [Recipient Name],  
>   
> Thank you for your invitation to discuss **[topic]**. I have confirmed the meeting for **[day, date, time]** in **[location/virtual platform]**. Please let me know if any adjustments are needed.  
>   
> Best regards,  
> **[Executive’s Name]** – [Title]  
> *[Assistant’s Name], Executive Assistant*  

*Why this works:* It acknowledges the request, restates the agreed details, and offers a quick correction path—standard practice for senior‑level correspondence [1].

---

### 2. Declining a Request Politely  

> **Subject:** Re: [Request Subject]  
>   
> Dear [Requester],  
>   
> I appreciate you bringing **[request]** to my attention. At this time, the executive’s schedule does not permit a meeting/commitment on this matter. I will keep your request on file and revisit it should circumstances change.  
>   
> Thank you for your understanding.  
>   
> Sincerely,  
> **[Executive’s Name]** – [Title]  
> *[Assistant’s Name], Executive Assistant*  

*Why this works:* Provides a clear, courteous decline while leaving the door open for future consideration [1].

---

### 3. Follow‑up After a Call  

> **Subject:** Follow‑up & Next Steps – [Call Topic]  
>   
> Hi [Name],  
>   
> Thank you for the productive conversation earlier today. As discussed, the executive would like to move forward with the following actions:  
>   
> 1. **[Action 1]** – responsible: [Person] – deadline: [Date]  
> 2. **[Action 2]** – responsible: [Person] – deadline: [Date]  
>   
> Please confirm receipt and let us know if any clarification is required.  
>   
> Best,  
> **[Executive’s Name]** – [Title]  
> *[Assistant’s Name], Executive Assistant*  

*Why this works:* Summarizes decisions, assigns accountability, and prompts confirmation—key for senior‑level follow‑ups [1].

---

### 4. Thank‑You Note After an Event  

> **Subject:** Thank You – [Event/Conference]  
>   
> Dear [Host/Organizer],  
>   
> On behalf of **[Executive’s Name]**, I extend sincere thanks for the warm hospitality and insightful sessions at **[event name]**. The executive found the discussions on **[specific topic]** especially valuable and looks forward to future collaboration.  
>   
> Warm regards,  
> **[Executive’s Name]** – [Title]  
> *[Assistant’s Name], Executive Assistant*  

*Why this works:* Shows appreciation, references a specific highlight, and reinforces the relationship—common practice for senior executives [1].

---

### 5. Request for Information / Documents  

> **Subject:** Request – [Document/Information] Needed by [Date]  
>   
> Dear [Colleague/Team],  
>   
> To prepare for **[upcoming meeting/decision]**, the executive requires the following items by **[deadline]**:  
>   
> - **[Item 1]**  
> - **[Item 2]**  
>   
> Please forward the materials to me at your earliest convenience. Let me know if any obstacles arise.  
>   
> Thank you,  
> **[Executive’s Name]** – [Title]  
> *[Assistant’s Name], Executive Assistant*  

*Why this works:* Clearly lists deliverables, sets a deadline, and offers a point of contact for issues—efficient for high‑level workflows [1].
