# Research: As a Product Designer, critique designs against the user's job-to-be-done — not personal taste

**Why the critique should be anchored to the user’s Job‑to‑Be‑Done (JTBD)**  

A product is “something that can be seen or experienced, created to meet the needs or desires of consumers” — it exists to solve a real problem for a real person, not to satisfy the designer’s aesthetic preferences [1]. When a design is evaluated against the JTBD, the reviewer is asking the same question that defines a successful product: *Does this solution fulfill the specific outcome the user is trying to achieve?*  

**How to shift from “personal taste” to “JTBD‑focused” feedback**

| Step | What to do | Why it matters (JTBD lens) |
|------|------------|----------------------------|
| 1. **State the JTBD explicitly** | Write the user’s functional, social and emotional goal in one sentence (e.g., “When I’m commuting, I want to check my calendar without taking my phone out, so I feel in control of my schedule”). | Makes the success criteria concrete; every design element can be mapped to it. |
| 2. **Map design elements to JTBD outcomes** | For each major UI/UX decision, note the intended outcome (e.g., “Glanceable card shows next meeting → reduces cognitive load”). | Shows whether a feature is *purpose‑driven* or merely decorative. |
| 3. **Measure against JTBD success metrics** | Use quantitative (time to complete, error rate) or qualitative (user confidence) indicators that directly reflect the job. | Evidence‑based critique replaces “I don’t like the colour” with “The colour contrast increases glance time by 30 % and harms task speed.” |
| 4. **Prioritise gaps over likes** | Highlight where the design fails to meet the JTBD (missing information, extra steps, unclear affordance) before commenting on style. | Aligns the team’s effort with the product’s market‑fit imperative (“Products should fulfill a real need or want in the market” [1]). |
| 5. **Frame personal preferences as secondary** | If a taste issue could impact JTBD (e.g., a font that reduces readability), surface it as a *risk to the job*; otherwise, treat it as a “nice‑to‑have” note. | Keeps the conversation focused on value delivery, not subjective aesthetics. |

**Practical example**

*Design:* A mobile onboarding screen uses a pastel gradient background and a large hero illustration.  
*JTBD:* “I need to understand how to set up my health‑tracking profile in under 2 minutes, so I can start using the app immediately.”  

*JTBD‑focused critique:*  
- **Information hierarchy** – The gradient reduces contrast, making the “Next” button harder to spot; this adds ~5 seconds to the task, jeopardising the 2‑minute goal.  
- **Illustration relevance** – The hero image does not convey any step of the profile setup; it consumes visual bandwidth without supporting the job.  
- **Optional style note** – The colour palette is pleasant, but could be swapped for a higher‑contrast scheme without harming the brand tone.

**Bottom line**  
Critiquing against the JTBD turns design review into a product‑validation exercise: every comment is tied to whether the solution delivers the outcome the user hired it for. This aligns with the core definition of a product as “something … created to meet the needs or desires of consumers” and ensures that design work drives market fit, not personal taste [1].
