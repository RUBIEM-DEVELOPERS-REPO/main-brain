# Research: risk assessment based on client history financial data property details health information

**How to combine client‑history, financial, property and health data into a single risk‑assessment framework**

| Step | What to collect | Why it matters | Tools / Methods |
|------|----------------|----------------|-----------------|
| 1. Data‑capture | • Past loss/claim history (property, liability, health)  <br>• Financial health (cash‑flow, credit, revenue stability)  <br>• Property specifics (location, construction, hazard exposure)  <br>• Health information (pre‑existing conditions, utilization trends) | These four data streams are the core inputs that insurers use to estimate the *likelihood* and *financial impact* of a loss — the two pillars of risk assessment [1]. | • CRM systems (e.g., Applied Epic, HawkSoft) to aggregate client‑application data  <br>• GIS platforms for geospatial property risk mapping  <br>• Catastrophe‑model software (RMS, AIR) for natural‑hazard loss estimates  <br>• Healthcare risk‑matrix (DMRA) to score clinical/financial consequences [2, 3] |
| 2. Quantitative scoring | Assign a **probability** score (e.g., 1‑5) for each hazard based on historical frequency and exposure (GIS + cat‑model outputs).  <br>Assign an **impact** score (e.g., 1‑5) using financial metrics (cash‑flow volatility, debt ratios) and health‑cost projections (expected treatment costs, morbidity rates). | Combining probability × impact yields a risk‑level matrix that highlights which exposures need immediate controls [2]. | • Risk matrix (decision‑matrix risk assessment, DMRA) – standard ISO 31000 tool used in healthcare and adaptable to insurance [2]. |
| 3. Qualitative overlay | Review “soft” factors:  • Management practices (business continuity plans, safety programs)  <br>• Liability culture (training, incident reporting)  <br>• Health‑policy compliance (HIPAA, clinical protocols). | Qualitative judgment refines the numeric score and captures emerging threats that models may miss [1]. |
| 4. Prioritisation & action | Rank exposures from highest to lowest risk‑level.  <br>For top‑tier risks, develop mitigation actions:  <br>• Property upgrades (fire suppression, flood barriers)  <br>• Financial safeguards (cash‑reserve policies, credit‑line reviews)  <br>• Health interventions (preventive care programs, chronic‑disease management). | Targeted controls reduce both frequency and severity, leading to lower premiums and fewer claim disputes [1]. |
| 5. Continuous monitoring | Feed new claim data, financial statements, property inspections and health utilization reports back into the matrix quarterly. | Ongoing updates keep the assessment current and support dynamic pricing or coverage adjustments [1]. |

### Key take‑aways

* **Integrated view:** Insurance risk assessment already blends geographic, operational and financial elements; adding a health‑risk matrix (DMRA) extends the same probability‑impact logic to clinical data [2, 3].
* **Tool synergy:** GIS + catastrophe models quantify property exposure; CRM & financial‑analysis software quantify cash‑flow risk; a risk‑matrix translates health data into comparable impact scores.
* **Outcome:** A single, evidence‑based risk‑level score per client that informs underwriting, premium setting, and targeted loss‑prevention recommendations.
