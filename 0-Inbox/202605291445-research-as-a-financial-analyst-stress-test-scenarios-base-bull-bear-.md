# Research: As a Financial Analyst, stress-test scenarios (base / bull / bear) before presenting a recommendation

**Stress‑testing a financial recommendation (base / bull / bear)**  

Below is a practical, evidence‑grounded workflow you can follow as a Financial Analyst. It draws on core finance concepts—risk management, scenario analysis, and cash‑flow modelling—as described in the Investopedia overview of finance [3].

| Step | What to do | Why it matters |
|------|------------|----------------|
| **1. Define the base case** | • Build a detailed financial model (income statement, balance sheet, cash‑flow) using the most likely assumptions for revenue growth, margins, capital expenditures, financing costs, and macro‑economic inputs. <br>• Validate the model against historical performance and any management guidance. | The base case is the reference point against which the bull and bear outcomes are measured. |
| **2. Identify key risk drivers** | • List the variables that have the greatest impact on valuation (e.g., sales volume, price elasticity, cost of goods sold, interest rates, foreign‑exchange rates). <br>• Use sensitivity analysis (one‑at‑a‑time) to rank them by effect on EBITDA, free cash flow, or valuation metric. | Focusing on the most material levers ensures the stress‑test is realistic and not overly broad. |
| **3. Construct the bull scenario** | • Choose optimistic but plausible shifts for the top risk drivers (e.g., +15 % revenue growth, -5 % operating margin compression, lower financing costs). <br>• Adjust macro assumptions (strong GDP growth, favorable regulatory environment). <br>• Re‑run the model to obtain revised cash‑flows and valuation. | The bull case shows upside potential and helps gauge the maximum upside that investors might expect. |
| **4. Construct the bear scenario** | • Apply adverse shifts (e.g., –20 % revenue, +10 % cost of goods sold, higher interest rates, currency depreciation). <br>• Incorporate downside macro events (recession, supply‑chain disruption). <br>• Re‑run the model for the downside cash‑flows and valuation. | The bear case reveals vulnerability and the worst‑case loss exposure, a core element of risk management. |
| **5. Quantify the impact** | • Compare key outputs across the three cases: revenue, EBITDA, free‑cash‑flow, net‑present‑value (NPV) or internal‑rate‑of‑return (IRR). <br>• Express the spread as a percentage change from the base case. | Clear, numeric differences make it easy for stakeholders to see the range of possible outcomes. |
| **6. Stress‑test balance‑sheet resilience** | • Model the effect of each scenario on liquidity ratios (current ratio, cash‑conversion cycle) and leverage (debt‑to‑EBITDA). <br>• Check covenant compliance under the bear case. | Ensures the firm can survive adverse conditions without breaching debt covenants or liquidity thresholds. |
| **7. Document assumptions & sources** | • Create a concise “Assumptions Table” for each scenario. <br>• Cite the source of macro data (e.g., IMF, World Bank) and any internal forecasts. | Transparency builds credibility and lets reviewers trace the logic. |
| **8. Prepare the recommendation** | • Summarize the three outcomes in a slide or memo (e.g., base NPV = $X m; bull = +$Y m; bear = ‑$Z m). <br>• State your view (buy/hold/sell) and the primary risk that could flip the recommendation. | A succinct, data‑driven recommendation is easier for decision‑makers to act on. |

### Quick Checklist for Presentation

- **Base case**: most likely assumptions, validated model.  
- **Bull case**: optimistic but credible upside (e.g., +10‑15 % revenue).  
- **Bear case**: realistic downside (e.g., –15‑20 % revenue, higher costs).  
- **Key metrics**: revenue, EBITDA, free‑cash‑flow, NPV/IRR, liquidity & leverage ratios.  
- **Assumptions table**: side‑by‑side comparison.  
- **Risk narrative**: what could cause a shift from base to bear (or bull).  

By following this structured approach—grounded in fundamental finance principles of budgeting, forecasting, and risk management—you’ll be able to stress‑test any investment or corporate finance proposal and present a clear, defensible recommendation.
