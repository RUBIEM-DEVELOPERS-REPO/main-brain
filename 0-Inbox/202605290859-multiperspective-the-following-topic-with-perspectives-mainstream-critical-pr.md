The question asks how digital platforms affect news and journalistic content, examined from four analytical stand‑points – mainstream, critical, practitioner and recent – by locating the most prominent web sources for each perspective and summarising their claims.

## Perspectives  

### mainstream  
The Centre for Media Transition’s 2018 report, commissioned by the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission, outlines four themes for assessing digital platforms’ impact on news: the public function of content, choice/diversity, consumption/customisation, and quality changes [2]. It concludes that platforms reshape both the supply chain and audience access, producing mixed effects on plurality and quality, but stops short of recommending regulation [2].

### practitioner

### recent

## Cross‑cutting themes  
- **Platform‑driven change to choice and diversity** – all available sources (the 2018 report) note that digital platforms alter how news is produced, distributed and consumed, affecting audience choice [2].  
- **Quality concerns** – the report flags potential declines or shifts in journalistic quality linked to platform algorithms and monetisation models [2].  
- **Lack of recent empirical data** – despite a “recent” perspective, the most up‑to‑date publicly available analysis remains the 2018 study, indicating a gap in current literature [12][13][2].

## Open questions  
- **Critical assessment gap** – what do critical scholars argue about power asymmetries, platform capture, or democratic harms? (No critical source found).  
- **Practitioner insights** – how do newsrooms actually adapt workflows, revenue models, or editorial standards in response to platforms? (No practitioner case study located).  
- **Latest evidence** – have platform impacts intensified, lessened, or changed form since 2018, especially with the rise of AI‑generated content? (Recent sources missing).  

## Bottom line  
The only substantive evidence retrieved is a 2018 academic report that maps digital platforms’ influence on news choice, diversity, consumption and quality. While it provides a solid baseline, the absence of distinct critical, practitioner, and up‑to‑date recent sources means the analysis cannot capture contemporary debates, on‑the‑ground newsroom adaptations, or newer scholarly critiques. Consequently, any synthesis must be qualified: current understanding is anchored in pre‑2020 research, and further investigation is needed to fill the critical, practical, and recent evidence gaps.
