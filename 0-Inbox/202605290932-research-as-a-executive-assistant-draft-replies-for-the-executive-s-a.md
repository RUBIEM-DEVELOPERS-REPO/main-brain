# Research: As a Executive Assistant, draft replies for the executive's approval — never pretend to be them

**How to Draft Executive Replies for Approval (Without Pretending to Be the Executive)**  

1. **Start with a clear “From” line**  
   - Use the executive’s name and title (e.g., *John Smith, Chief Operating Officer*). This signals that the draft is *for* the executive, not *by* the assistant.

2. **Adopt the executive’s tone and style**  
   - Review previous emails from the executive to capture their preferred level of formality, use of greetings, and signature block.  
   - Keep the language concise and decision‑focused, reflecting the executive’s role as a senior decision‑maker who “makes decisions and puts them into action” [1].

3. **Structure the draft**  
   - **Greeting** – polite but brief (e.g., “Dear Team,” or “Hi Maria,”).  
   - **Purpose statement** – one sentence that states why you are writing.  
   - **Key points / decisions** – bullet points or short paragraphs that outline the executive’s position, any required actions, and deadlines.  
   - **Call to action** – specify what you need from the recipient (e.g., confirmation, feedback, a report).  
   - **Closing** – a courteous sign‑off that matches the executive’s usual style (“Best regards,” “Thanks,” etc.).

4. **Insert placeholders for the executive’s input**  
   - Use brackets or highlighted text (e.g., *[Insert final figure]*, *[Confirm date]*). This makes it obvious that the draft still requires the executive’s review.

5. **Add a brief “For your approval” note**  
   - At the top or bottom of the draft, write a short line such as:  
     *“Please review and approve the text below before it is sent.”*  
   - This reinforces that the assistant is seeking permission, not speaking on the executive’s behalf.

6. **Proofread for accuracy and consistency**  
   - Verify names, dates, figures, and any referenced documents.  
   - Ensure the draft aligns with the executive’s known preferences and the organization’s branding.

7. **Send the draft with a concise cover note**  
   - Example:  
     ```
     Hi John,

     Please find attached the draft reply to the client’s request for pricing. Let me know if any changes are needed or if I should send it as‑is.

     Thanks,
     [Your Name]
     Executive Assistant
     ```

**Key principle:** The assistant’s role is to *prepare* the communication for the executive’s signature, not to *impersonate* the executive. By clearly labeling the draft, using the executive’s established voice, and requesting explicit approval, you respect both the executive’s authority and professional ethics.
