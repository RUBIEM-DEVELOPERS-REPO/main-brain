# Multi-perspective research: Do deep multi-perspective research on the history of cartography across 6 regions with sources

## Topic statement
The history of cartography across diverse global regions—including Europe, the Mediterranean, Islamic and South Asian societies, East and Southeast Asian societies, and traditional African, American, Arctic, Australian, and Pacific societies—reflects a complex evolution from artistic and cognitive spatial representations to highly structured scientific and digital tools [1, 11]. This research note synthesizes multiple perspectives on how maps function as cognitive systems, material artifacts, and social constructions across these regions [1]. It examines how historical cartography is analyzed through mainstream academic, critical, practical, and recent technological lenses.

## Perspectives

### Mainstream
The mainstream perspective, exemplified by the landmark *History of Cartography* project, views maps through three primary lenses: cognitive systems, material culture, and social constructions [1]. It defines maps broadly as graphic representations that facilitate a spatial understanding of things, concepts, conditions, processes, or events [1]. This perspective traces regional developments, noting that while ancient Greek cartography relied heavily on geometry and astronomy, Western medieval mapping prioritized artistic and aesthetic approaches to communicate religious and mythical beliefs [2]. Furthermore, it highlights that in regions like traditional Africa, the Americas, the Arctic, Australia, and the Pacific, a lack of surviving physical artifacts requires scholars to emphasize cognitive and social approaches over material ones [1].

### Critical
The critical perspective emphasizes that cartography is an art deeply shaped by the political, social, and cultural climates of its production [6]. Critical analyses of historical maps reveal how cartographers' regional biases and interests dictate what information is included, omitted, or distorted [6]. For example, Martin Waldseemüller’s 1507 world map depicts Europe with high geographic accuracy and density while leaving regions like Africa and North America highly distorted or blank, reflecting a Eurocentric worldview [6]. Similarly, Mateus Prunes' 1559 Mediterranean chart challenges modern conventions by orienting West at the top and incorporating rich, culturally specific illustrations of regional leaders, flags, and mythical animals [6].

### Practitioner
Practitioners focus on the utility of historical maps for researching land ownership, changing landscapes, and cultural land use [6]. They view mapmaking as a balance of science and art, where design choices like relief shading, color coding, and selective omission (such as in 19th-century bird's-eye cityscapes) are used to clarify spatial information [10]. Modern practitioners leverage digital tools like GIS and Google Earth to overlay historical maps with satellite imagery [10]. However, they face challenges because map users and digital processing developers often work in disciplinary silos [9]. To bridge this gap, practitioners are developing semi-automatic, automatic, and deep learning technologies to extract features from scanned historical maps and link them to structured databases [9].
