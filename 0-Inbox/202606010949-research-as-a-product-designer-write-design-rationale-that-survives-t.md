# Research: As a Product Designer, write design rationale that survives the review meeting

**Design Rationale that Holds Up in a Review Meeting**

| Element | What to state | Why it survives scrutiny |
|---------|---------------|--------------------------|
| **Problem statement** | Begin with a concise, data‑driven description of the user need or business goal you are solving. Quote the metric or user quote that triggered the work. | Reviewers can see the *why* immediately; it ties the design to a measurable objective rather than a personal preference. |
| **Design goals & success criteria** | List 2‑4 concrete goals (e.g., reduce task‑completion time by 15 %, increase NPS for this flow from 45 to 60). | Clear, testable criteria give the panel a way to judge the outcome later, turning the rationale into a future‑validation plan. |
| **User‑centered evidence** | Summarize the most relevant research (user interviews, usability tests, analytics) and attach the artefacts (e.g., affinity‑map, heat‑maps). | Evidence‑backed decisions are hard to dispute; reviewers can trace each design choice back to a user insight. |
| **Design alternatives & trade‑offs** | Briefly show 2‑3 explored concepts, why they were rejected (cost, technical risk, low‑impact on the goal), and why the chosen solution wins. | Demonstrates thorough exploration and rational decision‑making, pre‑empting “why didn’t you consider X?” questions. |
| **Alignment with product strategy** | Reference the product’s positioning, market fit, and unique value proposition (e.g., “supports our premium‑segment differentiation”). | Shows the design is not an isolated UI tweak but a strategic move that reinforces the overall product vision. |
| **Technical feasibility & constraints** | Cite the engineering estimate (e.g., “adds 0.2 s load time, within the 0.5 s budget”) and any platform limits you respected. | Shows you have collaborated with engineering and that the design is realistic, reducing push‑back on implementation risk. |
| **Impact & measurement plan** | Define how you will measure success after launch (A/B test metrics, analytics dashboards, user‑feedback loops). | Gives reviewers confidence that the design’s effectiveness will be validated, turning the rationale into an actionable roadmap. |
| **Risks & mitigations** | List the top 2‑3 risks (e.g., “possible confusion for legacy users”) and the mitigation steps (guided onboarding, incremental rollout). | Anticipating objections demonstrates foresight and reduces the perception of hidden problems. |

### How to Present It

1. **One‑page slide** – headline, bullet‑point rationale, and a small visual of the final design.  
2. **Appendix** – detailed research artefacts, cost/effort estimates, and the measurement plan.  
3. **Narrative flow** – problem → evidence → alternatives → chosen solution → impact → risks.  

### Why This Structure Works

* It **grounds every claim in evidence** (user research, metrics, engineering data), which reviewers can verify.  
* It **shows systematic exploration** (alternatives & trade‑offs), preventing accusations of “design by fiat.”  
* It **links design to business outcomes** (product strategy, success criteria), satisfying stakeholders focused on ROI.  
* It **pre‑emptively addresses objections** (risks, feasibility), reducing the chance of a “let’s revisit this later” outcome.

> The definition of a product emphasizes that it is created through *design, production, and distribution* to satisfy a need [1]. By mirroring that lifecycle—identifying the need, designing with evidence, and planning distribution/measurement—you align the design rationale with the very nature of a product, making the rationale harder to reject.
