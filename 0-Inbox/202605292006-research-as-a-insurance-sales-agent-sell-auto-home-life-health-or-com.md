**How to Sell Auto, Home, Life, Health, or Commercial Insurance – A Quick Playbook**

| Step | What to Do | Why It Works (Evidence) |
|------|------------|--------------------------|
| **1. Understand the Core Product Elements** | Know the **premium**, **deductible**, and **policy limit** for each line of coverage. Be ready to explain how each affects price and protection. | These three components are the “core components of most insurance policies” and are the primary levers customers compare when choosing a policy【1†L15-L22】. |
| **2. Identify the Customer’s Needs** | Ask targeted questions: <br>• Auto – driving history, vehicle value, usage patterns.<br>• Home – property value, location, replacement cost.<br>• Life – age, dependents, financial goals.<br>• Health – age, health status, coverage preferences.<br>• Commercial – industry risks, employee count, regulatory requirements. | Insurance is purchased to **provide financial protection** against specific losses (accidents, injury, property damage) and the right coverage matches the risk profile【1†L31-L38】. |
| **3. Position the Value Proposition** | Translate product features into tangible benefits: <br>• “Lower deductible = lower out‑of‑pocket when a claim occurs.”<br>• “Higher policy limit protects you from catastrophic loss.”<br>• “Bundling auto & home can reduce overall premium.” | Customers care about **financial protection** and cost; explaining how premiums, deductibles, and limits affect both helps them see value【1†L15-L23】. |
| **4. Leverage Mandatory Coverage Rules** | Highlight any legal requirements (e.g., auto liability in every state, health coverage under the ACA). This creates urgency and a baseline need. | Some policies, **such as auto or health insurance, may be mandatory**【1†L45-L48】. |
| **5. Use Comparative Quotes** | Generate at least **two–three quotes** from different carriers. Show side‑by‑side tables of premium, deductible, limit, and any discounts (multi‑policy, safe driver, etc.). | “Different insurers may charge different premiums for similar policies… finding the price that is right for you requires some legwork.”【1†L55-L61】 |
| **6. Address Objections with Data** | If price is a concern, explain how **risk factors** (age, claims history, credit) drive premiums and how improving those factors can lower cost over time. | Premiums are set based on **underwriting factors** such as claims history, age, location, etc.【1†L41-L45】 |
| **7. Close the Sale** | Summarize the chosen plan, confirm the **effective date**, collect the first premium payment, and schedule a follow‑up review (usually annually). | Insurance policies are **contracts** that begin once the premium is paid and the policy is issued; regular reviews keep coverage aligned with life changes. |
| **8. Post‑Sale Service** | Provide a clear **claims process** overview, contact information, and an online portal link. Good service leads to referrals and renewals. | Ongoing relationship management is a key differentiator for agents and brokers. |

### Quick Script Example (Auto)

> “Based on your clean driving record and the $25,000 value of your sedan, we can offer a **$120/month premium** with a **$500 deductible** and a **$100,000 liability limit**. If you’d like extra peace of mind, we can raise the limit to $300,000 for an additional $15/month. Bundling this with your home policy would shave $10 off the monthly cost. Since auto insurance is required in every state, this coverage keeps you compliant and financially protected.”

### Tips for All Lines

- **Personalize**: Tie coverage to the client’s specific assets (home value, business inventory, family needs).  
- **Educate**: Explain jargon (e.g., “policy limit” = maximum insurer payout).  
- **Follow‑up**: Annual check‑ins capture life changes (new car, marriage, business expansion) and open upsell opportunities.  

By grounding each conversation in the three universal policy components and the customer’s unique risk profile, you can confidently sell any of the five major insurance types.
