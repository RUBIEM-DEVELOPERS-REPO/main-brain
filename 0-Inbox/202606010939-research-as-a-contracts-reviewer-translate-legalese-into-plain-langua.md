# Research: As a Contracts Reviewer, translate legalese into plain language summaries non-lawyers can act on

**How to turn contract “legalese” into plain‑language summaries that anyone can act on**

| Step | What to do | Why it matters (plain‑language tip) | Example |
|------|------------|--------------------------------------|---------|
| **1. Identify the core elements** | Look for the five classic contract building blocks: **offer, acceptance, consideration, mutual assent, legality**【1†L13-L20】. | Explain each in everyday terms (“what one side promises, what the other side gives, and that both agree it’s a real deal”). | *Offer*: “Company A will pay $5,000.” |
| **2. Spot the “who, what, when, where, how much”** | Pull out the parties, the thing being exchanged, deadlines, and any amounts. | People need to know *who does what* and *by when* without deciphering jargon. | “You (the freelancer) will deliver the website by June 30 and get paid $3,000.” |
| **3. Translate each clause** | • **Force‑Majeure** → “If a natural disaster or war stops work, the deadline can be extended.” <br>• **Indemnification** → “If someone sues because of your mistake, you’ll pay the costs.” <br>• **Arbitration** → “Disagreements will be settled by a neutral third‑party, not a court.” <br>• **Termination** → “Either side can end the agreement early if they give 30 days’ notice.” | Use short, active sentences; replace Latin or legal terms with everyday equivalents. |
| **4. Highlight actions required** | List every step the reader must take (sign, deliver, pay, notify). | Action items are the “to‑do” list that non‑lawyers can follow. | “1️⃣ Sign and return the last page by May 5. 2️⃣ Send the first draft by May 20.” |
| **5. Flag risks & exceptions** | Note any “but” statements (e.g., “unless the client breaches”) and what they mean in plain talk. | Makes the reader aware of conditions that could change their obligations. | “You only have to finish the work if the client pays the first $1,000 on time.” |
| **6. Keep it short** | Aim for **one‑paragraph summaries** for each major section; use bullet points for details. | Readers lose focus after a few sentences; brevity drives compliance. |
| **7. Use visual cues** | Bold the parties’ names, dates, and dollar amounts; add check‑boxes for each action. | Visual hierarchy guides the eye to the most important facts. |
| **8. Add a “What‑if” FAQ** | Anticipate common questions (“What if the deadline is missed?”) and answer in plain language. | Reduces follow‑up queries and clarifies expectations. |

### Quick Template (fill‑in‑the‑blank)

> **Agreement Summary** – *[Contract Title]*  
> **Parties:** *[Your Company]* and *[Other Party]*  
> **What’s being exchanged:** *[Service or product]* for *[price or consideration]*.  
> **Key Dates:** *[Start date]* → *[Completion/Delivery date]*.  
> **Your responsibilities:**  
> - *[Action 1]* by *[date]*  
> - *[Action 2]* by *[date]*  
> **Their responsibilities:**  
> - *[Action A]* by *[date]*  
> - *[Action B]* by *[date]*  
> **Special clauses (plain language):**  
> - *Force‑Majeure:* “If a natural disaster stops work, we’ll agree on a new deadline.”  
> - *Termination:* “Either side can end this deal with 30 days’ written notice.”  
> **Risks to watch:** *[Brief risk description]*.  

### Why this works
- **Grounded in contract fundamentals** – the five essential elements from the legal definition ensure you don’t miss a required piece【1†L13-L20】.  
- **Action‑oriented** – non‑lawyers need clear “what to do” steps, not abstract rights and duties.  
- **Plain‑language equivalents** for common clauses keep the summary understandable without losing legal effect.

Use this process for every contract you review, and you’ll produce concise, actionable summaries that non‑lawyers can read, understand, and follow.
