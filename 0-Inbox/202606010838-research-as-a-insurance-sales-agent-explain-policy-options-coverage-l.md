# Research: As a Insurance Sales Agent, explain policy options, coverage limits, premiums, and exclusions

**Insurance Policy Basics for a Sales Agent**

| Topic | What it means | Typical examples (from Atlas Insurance) |
|-------|---------------|----------------------------------------|
| **Policy options** | The different types of cover you can sell. Each option is a separate contract that addresses a specific risk. | Motor, Travel, Home, HomeLet, Pet, Boat, Health & Dental, Life & Pensions, Business, iCycle, Employee Assistance [1] |
| **Coverage limits** | The maximum amount the insurer will pay for a covered loss under the policy. Limits are set per incident (e.g., per claim) and/or in aggregate for the policy term. | A motor policy might limit liability to € 1 million per accident; a home policy might cap building coverage at the replacement‑cost value of the house. Atlas lists the product lines but does not publish exact limits, so you must obtain the specific limit tables from the carrier’s policy wording. |
| **Premiums** | The price the policyholder pays for the coverage, usually expressed as an annual or monthly amount. Premiums are calculated from: <br>• Risk exposure (type of risk, value of the insured item) <br>• Coverage limits and deductibles <br>• Policyholder characteristics (age, driving record, health, claims history) <br>• Geographic and regulatory factors | Atlas’s “I’d like a quote” flow shows that premiums are generated per product (Motor, Health & Dental, Business, etc.) after the applicant provides relevant data. Exact premium formulas are proprietary and not disclosed on the public site. |
| **Exclusions** | Specific situations or conditions that are *not* covered, even if they fall within the general scope of the policy. Exclusions protect the insurer from paying for high‑frequency or uninsurable risks. | Common exclusions (though not listed on the Atlas site) include: intentional damage, wear‑and‑tear, use of a vehicle for racing, pre‑existing medical conditions, damage caused by war or nuclear events. You must review each policy’s “Exclusions” section to explain them to the client. |

### How to Present These Elements to a Client  

1. **Identify the client’s needs** – ask about assets, activities, and risk tolerance.  
2. **Match a policy option** – choose the product line that aligns (e.g., Motor for a driver, HomeLet for a landlord).  
3. **Explain the coverage limit** – show the dollar/€ amount the insurer will pay and how it relates to the client’s exposure.  
4. **Quote the premium** – run the insurer’s rating engine (Atlas’s “I’d like a quote” tool) and present the cost, noting any discounts (e.g., multi‑policy bundling).  
5. **Highlight key exclusions** – list the most relevant exclusions so the client knows what is *not* covered and can decide whether to add endorsements or higher limits.  

### Practical Tips for an Insurance Sales Agent  

- **Use the insurer’s product pages** (e.g., Atlas’s list of services) as a starting point, then pull the detailed policy documents for limits, premiums, and exclusions.  
- **Ask for a written schedule** that spells out the limit per item and any aggregate caps.  
- **Run “what‑if” scenarios** to illustrate how a higher limit or lower deductible changes the premium.  
- **Document exclusions** in plain language; clients often assume “all‑risk” coverage, which is rarely true.  

> **Bottom line:** Policy options define *what* you can insure; coverage limits define *how much* the insurer will pay; premiums are the price of that protection; and exclusions define *what is left out*. Always back up your sales pitch with the specific wording from the carrier’s policy booklet.  

*Sources:* Atlas Insurance product list shows the range of policy options available to sell [1]. No detailed limit, premium, or exclusion data is published on the site, so the explanation relies on standard industry practice.
