**How a Contracts Reviewer can spot the high‑risk clauses you listed**

| Clause type | What to look for | Typical red‑flag language | Why it matters |
|-------------|------------------|---------------------------|----------------|
| **Cap** (liability limitation) | • A dollar amount or “the greater of …” that limits one party’s liability.<br>• Exceptions to the cap (e.g., for fraud, gross negligence). | “Liability shall not exceed $ X, except for breaches of confidentiality.” | Caps can leave the company exposed to uncapped losses if the exception is broad. |
| **Indemnity** | • One‑sided indemnification (the other party bears all risk).<br>• “Broad” or “unlimited” language, especially for third‑party claims.<br>• Lack of carve‑outs for the indemnitor’s own negligence. | “The Supplier shall indemnify, defend and hold harmless the Company from any and all claims…” | Overly broad indemnities can create uncontrolled exposure and costly litigation. |
| **IP (Intellectual Property)** | • Ownership of pre‑existing IP vs. newly created IP.<br>• License scope (territory, field‑of‑use, sublicensing).<br>• Moral‑rights waivers or non‑assertion clauses. | “All inventions, improvements, and works‑made‑for‑hire created under this Agreement shall be the exclusive property of the Client.” | Mis‑aligned IP ownership can strip the company of valuable rights or force unwanted licensing. |
| **Auto‑renewal** | • Automatic extension terms (often “one‑year renewal unless notice is given”).<br>• Notice period length (e.g., 30 days vs. 90 days).<br>• Lack of price‑adjustment provisions for renewals. | “This Agreement shall automatically renew for successive one‑year terms unless either party provides written notice of non‑renewal at least 60 days prior to the expiration date.” | Auto‑renewals can lock the company into unfavorable terms or price increases without a chance to renegotiate. |
| **Term** | • Length of the initial term and any extensions.<br>• Early‑termination rights (for convenience, breach, or material change of control).<br>• Termination fees or penalties. | “The term shall be three (3) years, renewable automatically for additional one‑year periods, and may be terminated by either party for cause upon 30 days’ notice.” | A long, non‑terminable term can tie up resources; missing termination rights can make it costly to exit. |

### Practical checklist for each contract

1. **Read the “Definitions” section first** – many high‑risk clauses are cross‑referenced by defined terms (e.g., “Cap”, “Indemnified Parties”).
2. **Search the document** for keywords: *cap, limitation, indemnify, hold harmless, IP, intellectual property, ownership, license, auto‑renew, renewal, term, termination*.
3. **Mark the clause** and note:
   - Exact language (quote the sentence/paragraph).
   - Whether the clause is one‑sided or balanced.
   - Any exceptions or carve‑outs.
4. **Assess risk** using a simple rating (Low / Medium / High) based on:
   - Scope (e.g., unlimited vs. capped).
   - Counter‑party’s bargaining power.
   - Business impact if the clause is triggered.
5. **Add a comment** for the contract owner with:
   - The identified clause.
   - Why it is high‑risk.
   - Suggested mitigation (e.g., negotiate a higher cap, add mutual indemnity, limit auto‑renewal notice period, insert termination for convenience).

### Example comment

> **Clause:** Indemnification (Section 5.2) – “Supplier shall indemnify, defend and hold harmless Company from any and all claims, losses, damages, and expenses arising out of Supplier’s services.”  
> **Risk:** Unlimited, one‑sided indemnity with no carve‑out for Supplier’s negligence.  
> **Mitigation:** Limit indemnity to direct damages, add mutual indemnity, and carve out Supplier’s gross negligence only.
