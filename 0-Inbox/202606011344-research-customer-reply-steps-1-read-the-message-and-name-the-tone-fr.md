# Research: customer reply. Steps: 1) Read the message and name the tone (frustrated / confused / excited / neutral). 2) Identify the underlying need (often different from the literal ask). 3) Draft a reply that opens with the right acknowledgment, resolves the real need, attaches a date to any commitment. 4) Flag any churn-risk or expansion-signal language separately. No macro-speak

**Tone:** *frustrated*  

**Underlying need:** The customer is really looking for a concrete next‑step and a firm timeline – they want reassurance that their issue will be resolved quickly, not just an explanation.

**Proposed reply**

> Hi [Customer Name],  
>   
> I’m sorry you’ve had to deal with this – I understand how frustrating it is when things don’t work as expected. I’ve looked into the details and will have a fix ready for you by **Wednesday 12 May 2026**. I’ll send you the updated files and a short walkthrough on that day, and I’ll follow‑up on **Friday 14 May** to confirm everything is running smoothly.  
>   
> If anything changes in the meantime, please let me know right away. Thanks for your patience – we’ll get this sorted for you.  

**Churn‑risk / expansion signal**  
- The word *frustrated* and the request for a firm deadline indicate churn risk; the promise of a quick fix and follow‑up is a mitigation opportunity. No explicit upsell cue is present.
