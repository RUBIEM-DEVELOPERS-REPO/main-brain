# Research: As a Product Manager, run customer interviews focused on past behaviour, not hypothetical future

**How to run customer‑interview questions that surface *past behaviour* rather than “what‑if” speculation**

| Goal | Sample question style | Why it works (behaviour‑based) |
|------|----------------------|--------------------------------|
| **Identify real actions** | “Can you walk me through the last time you [performed the task / used a similar solution]?” | Forces the interviewee to recount a concrete episode instead of imagining a future scenario. |
| **Uncover triggers & context** | “What prompted you to start looking for a solution the last time you faced X?” | Reveals the situational cues that actually drove the behaviour. |
| **Map the decision flow** | “When you decided to choose Y, what alternatives did you consider and why did you reject them?” | Shows the real evaluation criteria that mattered at the moment. |
| **Measure frequency & effort** | “How often do you currently do Z, and how much time does it take?” | Gives quantitative signals (frequency, duration) that are observable, not imagined. |
| **Surface work‑arounds** | “When the tool didn’t work the way you wanted, what did you do instead?” | Highlights pain points and improvisations that indicate unmet needs. |
| **Capture outcomes & satisfaction** | “After you completed X, what was the result for you? How did you feel about it?” | Links behaviour to tangible outcomes, helping you gauge value. |
| **Validate assumptions** | “You mentioned you usually do Y in the mornings—what does a typical morning look like for you?” | Checks consistency across the interview and reduces hindsight bias. |

### Practical Tips for the Interview

1. **Start with a story prompt** – “Tell me about the most recent time you…”. Stories naturally anchor the conversation in the past.  
2. **Avoid leading “would you” or “if you could” phrasing** – these invite speculation.  
3. **Use “who, what, when, where, how” probes** after the initial story to dig deeper into each step.  
4. **Encourage concrete details** – ask for numbers, tools used, people involved, and exact locations.  
5. **Record verbatim quotes** – they preserve the original phrasing, which is useful for later synthesis.  
6. **Cross‑check within the same interview** – revisit earlier answers later (“Earlier you said you did X on Tuesdays; can you elaborate on that?”) to catch inconsistencies.  
7. **Limit the interview to 30‑45 minutes** – keeps recall fresh and reduces fatigue‑induced imagination.

### Why Past‑Behaviour Focus Beats Hypotheticals

* **Higher predictive validity** – People are far better at describing what they *actually* did than what they *might* do.  
* **Reveals hidden work‑arounds** – Real actions expose improvisations that never appear in “ideal‑scenario” questions.  
* **Provides concrete data** – Frequency, duration, and sequence details can be quantified for product‑roadmap prioritisation.  
* **Reduces bias** – Anchoring on a specific event mitigates the optimism bias that often skews future‑oriented answers.

---

**Bottom line:** Structure every interview around a recent, specific incident, drill down with “how/why” follow‑ups, and avoid any “what‑if” language. This approach yields reliable, action‑able insights that directly inform product decisions.
