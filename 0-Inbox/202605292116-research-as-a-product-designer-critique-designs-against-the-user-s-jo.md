# Research: As a Product Designer, critique designs against the user's job-to-be-done — not personal taste.

**How to Critique a Design — Focus on the User’s Job‑to‑Be‑Done, Not Your Personal Taste**

| Step | What to Look For | Why It Matters for the JTBD |
|------|------------------|----------------------------|
| **1. Identify the Core JTBD** | Ask: *What outcome is the user trying to achieve?* Is the design helping the user “get a task done faster”, “reduce effort”, “avoid error”, or “feel confident*?* | A product exists to satisfy a **real need or want** in the market [1]. If the design doesn’t map to that need, it fails the JTBD regardless of how “pretty” it looks. |
| **2. Measure Outcome‑Oriented Success** | Check if the design delivers the **desired result** (e.g., time saved, error reduction, increased satisfaction). Use metrics such as task‑completion time, error rate, or Net‑Promoter Score. | The JTBD framework treats the product as a **solution that produces a specific result**, not just a collection of features [1]. |
| **3. Evaluate Functional Fit** | Does every element (layout, interaction, visual cue) directly support the user’s goal? Remove or redesign anything that adds friction without adding value. | A product’s **features and quality** must align with the job; extraneous elements dilute focus and can harm usability [1]. |
| **4. Assess Emotional & Social Dimensions** | Beyond the functional task, consider how the design makes the user feel (confidence, control, status). Does it reinforce the user’s self‑image or social expectations? | Successful products often deliver **emotional value** that reinforces why the user chose the job in the first place [1]. |
| **5. Test Against Real‑World Context** | Observe users in their natural environment. Does the design work under the constraints (device, connectivity, lighting) they actually face? | The JTBD is **context‑dependent**; a solution that works in a lab may fail in the field. |
| **6. Prioritize Feedback Over Aesthetics** | Collect concrete user feedback (qualitative & quantitative). If users report that a visual style distracts from their goal, note it, but do not let personal preference dictate the verdict. | **Customer feedback** is the ultimate gauge of whether the product meets the job; personal taste is irrelevant [1]. |
| **7. Iterate with the JTBD Lens** | When revising, ask: *Will this change bring the user closer to completing their job?* If the answer is “no” or “unclear,” defer the change. | Iteration should be **goal‑driven**, not style‑driven. |

### Quick Checklist for a JTBD‑Focused Review  

- ☐ Does the design **directly enable** the user’s primary outcome?  
- ☐ Are **unnecessary visual flourishes** removed or minimized?  
- ☐ Are **error‑prevention** and **recovery** mechanisms built in?  
- ☐ Does the experience **reduce cognitive load** (clear hierarchy, obvious affordances)?  
- ☐ Are **emotional triggers** (trust, delight) aligned with the job’s motivations?  
- ☐ Have we **validated** each claim with user testing or metrics?  

### Bottom Line  
Treat every design decision as a hypothesis about how well it helps the user complete their **job‑to‑be‑done**. Evaluate with data, context, and the user’s desired outcome—not with your own aesthetic preferences. This keeps the product purpose‑centric, improves adoption, and ultimately delivers the value that defines a successful product [1].
