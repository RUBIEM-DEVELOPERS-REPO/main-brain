## JTBD‑Focused Design Critique Report  

**Purpose** – Provide a practical framework for product designers to evaluate designs against the user’s Job‑to‑Be‑Done (JTBD) rather than personal aesthetic preferences.  

### 1. Anchor the Review in the JTBD  
A product exists to solve a real problem for a real person, not to satisfy the designer’s taste [1]. The first step is to make the JTBD explicit, capturing functional, social, and emotional dimensions in a single sentence (e.g., “When I’m commuting, I want to check my calendar without taking my phone out, so I feel in control of my schedule”) [1][4].  

### 2. Map Design Elements to JTBD Outcomes  
For each major UI/UX decision, note the intended outcome that supports the JTBD (e.g., “Glanceable card shows next meeting → reduces cognitive load”). This mapping reveals whether a feature is purpose‑driven or merely decorative [1][4].  

### 3. Measure Against Success Metrics  
Select quantitative (time‑to‑complete, error rate) or qualitative (user confidence, perceived control) indicators that directly reflect the JTBD. Replace subjective comments (“I don’t like the colour”) with evidence‑based observations (“Low‑contrast background increases glance time by 30 % and slows task completion”) [1][4].  

### 4. Prioritise Gaps Over Preferences  
Identify design gaps that hinder the JTBD (missing information, extra steps, unclear affordance) before commenting on style. Treat aesthetic preferences as secondary; only flag them when they impact the job (e.g., a font that reduces readability) [4].  

### 5. Deliver Structured Feedback  
1. **JTBD Statement** – concise user goal.  
2. **Element‑Outcome Mapping** – table linking UI components to JTBD outcomes.  
3. **Metric Evaluation** – data or observations supporting success or failure.  
4. **Gap Prioritisation** – rank issues by impact on the JTBD.  
5. **Optional Style Notes** – brief, risk‑based comments on aesthetics.  

**Result** – A critique that is objective, outcome‑driven, and aligned with the product’s core purpose, ensuring design decisions are justified by user value rather than personal taste.  

*Assumed: the report will be used by design teams to standardise review sessions.*
