## Maintaining Voice Consistency Across Documentation, In‑App Text, and Marketing Hand‑offs  

### 1. Define a Unified Voice Charter  
- **Core attributes** – Identify 3‑5 adjectives that capture the brand’s personality (e.g., *clear, approachable, confident*).  
- **Tone matrix** – Map how each attribute shifts by audience and channel (e.g., more formal for API docs, conversational for UI tooltips, aspirational for marketing).  
- **Lexicon & style rules** – Compile preferred terminology, prohibited words, punctuation, and formatting conventions in a single style guide.  

### 2. Centralize the Reference Material  
- Store the voice charter, style guide, and examples in a shared repository (e.g., Confluence, Notion, or a version‑controlled markdown folder).  
- Tag each entry with the applicable surface (Docs, In‑App, Marketing) to aid quick lookup.  

### 3. Embed Voice Checks Into the Workflow  
- **Authoring tools** – Configure linters or plugins (e.g., Vale, Grammarly, or custom scripts) to flag deviations from the lexicon and tone rules.  
- **Review gates** – Require a “Voice Consistency” checklist item in PR or editorial reviews, confirming that the writer consulted the charter and that a peer has validated tone alignment.  

### 4. Create Cross‑Surface Templates  
- Provide ready‑made skeletons for common content types (release notes, onboarding screens, product brochures).  
- Each template should include placeholder text that demonstrates the desired voice, reducing the need for writers to “guess” the tone.  

### 5. Conduct Regular Audits & Training  
- Schedule quarterly audits that sample content from each surface, score it against the voice charter, and document findings.  
- Hold brief workshops to reinforce the voice principles and share audit results, ensuring continuous alignment as the product evolves.  

### 6. Facilitate Smooth Hand‑offs  
- When transitioning content from documentation to marketing (or vice‑versa), attach a “Voice Summary” that highlights any surface‑specific adjustments and the rationale behind them.  
- Use a shared ticketing system (e.g., Jira) to track hand‑off status and capture feedback loops.
