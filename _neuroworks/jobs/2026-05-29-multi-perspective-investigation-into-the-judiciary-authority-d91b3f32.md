**Judiciary Authority in Zimbabwe – Executive Summary**

Zimbabwe’s judiciary is constitutionally established, independent, and structured around a hierarchy that includes the Supreme Court, High Court, and subordinate courts. Judicial independence is enshrined in Section 79B of the Constitution, which prohibits any authority from directing judges except as provided by law. The Judicial Service Commission (JSC) appoints judges, sets tenure and remuneration, and outlines removal procedures. Recent political developments—particularly constitutional amendments and executive‑led reforms—have raised concerns about potential interference, but the legal framework still provides mechanisms for accountability and checks on executive power.

## 1. Historical Foundations

* Colonial legacy introduced a hybrid legal system combining Anglo‑Roman‑Dutch common law with indigenous customary law.  
* Pre‑colonial Shona and Ndebele chiefs exercised sovereign authority, later subsumed under colonial law.  
* Post‑independence retained much of the colonial structure, creating a plural system where legislation, case law, and customary law coexist.

## 2. Current Legal Framework

* **Constitution of Zimbabwe (2004, amended 2013)** – Supreme source of law; guarantees judicial independence (Sec. 79B).  
* **Judicial Service Commission** – Appoints judges, sets tenure and remuneration, prescribes removal procedures.  
* **Court Hierarchy** – Supreme Court (highest appellate authority), High Court (original jurisdiction and appellate), Administrative Court, and various special courts.

## 4. Recent Developments
