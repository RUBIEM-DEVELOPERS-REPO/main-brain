## Report: Judicial Authority in Zimbabwe

**Bottom line**  
Zimbabwe’s judiciary is the third branch of government, established by the Constitution of 1993 (amended 2013). It comprises the Supreme Court, Court of Appeal, High Court, and subordinate courts. Judicial appointments are made by the President on the recommendation of the Judicial Service Commission, and judges serve until the mandatory retirement age of 70. The Constitution guarantees judicial independence, protects judges from arbitrary removal, and sets their salaries by law. Recent reforms have focused on transparency, accountability, and a code of conduct, but concerns about political influence, case‑backlog, and resource constraints persist.  

## Current Legal Framework  
- **Constitutional basis**: The Constitution of Zimbabwe (1993, amended 2013) defines the structure, powers, and independence of the judiciary.  
- **Judicial Service Commission (JSC)**: Established in 2013 to oversee appointments, promotions, and disciplinary matters.  
- **Judicial appointments**: Made by the President on JSC recommendation; judges serve until age 70.  
- **Independence safeguards**: Provisions prevent arbitrary removal and ensure salary determination by law.

- **2013 JSC establishment**: Aimed to professionalise and depoliticise judicial appointments.

- **Ongoing challenges**: Political pressure, delays in case disposal, and limited court resources remain significant issues.
