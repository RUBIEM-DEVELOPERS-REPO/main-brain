## How to Draft Executive Replies for Approval (Without Pretending to Be the Executive)

1. **Identify the sender** – Begin the draft with the executive’s name and title in a “From” line (e.g., *John Smith, Chief Operating Officer*). This makes clear the reply is *for* the executive, not *by* the assistant.  

2. **Match the executive’s voice** – Review recent emails to capture preferred formality, greeting style, and signature block. Keep the language concise and decision‑focused, reflecting the executive’s role as a senior decision‑maker [1].  

3. **Structure the draft**  
   - **Greeting** – Polite and brief (e.g., “Dear Team,” or “Hi Maria,”).  
   - **Purpose statement** – One sentence stating why you are writing.  
   - **Key points / decisions** – Short paragraphs or bullet points that outline the executive’s position, required actions, and any deadlines.  
   - **Call to action** – Clearly specify what you need from the recipient (e.g., confirmation, feedback, a report).  
   - **Closing** – Use the executive’s usual sign‑off (“Best regards,” “Thanks,” etc.).  

4. **Insert placeholders for final input** – Use brackets or highlighted text such as *[Insert final figure]* or *[Confirm date]* so the executive can see where their review is required.  

5. **Add a “For your approval” note** – Place a brief line at the top or bottom of the draft, e.g., “Please review and approve the text below before it is sent.”  

6. **Proofread** – Verify names, dates, figures, and branding consistency before forwarding the draft to the executive.  

7. **Send with a concise cover note** – Example:  

   ```text
   Hi John,

   Please review the draft reply to the client’s pricing request. Let me know if any changes are needed or if I should send it as‑is.

   Thanks,
   [Your Name]
   ```
