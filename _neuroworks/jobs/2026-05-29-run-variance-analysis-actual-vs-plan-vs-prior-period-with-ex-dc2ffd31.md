**Answer**

The term *variance analysis* in financial reporting refers to the comparison of three numbers:

| Comparison | What it shows | Typical explanation |
|---|---|---|
|**Actual vs. Plan (Budget)**|How much the results differed from what was expected or budgeted.|A **favourable** (or positive) variance means the actual result was better than the plan (e.g., higher revenue or lower expense). An **unfavourable** (or negative) variance means the actual result was worse (e.g., lower revenue or higher expense).|
|**Actual vs. Prior Period**|How the current period’s performance compares with the same period in the previous year (or quarter).|A favourable variance indicates improvement over the prior period, while an unfavourable variance signals a decline.|
|**Plan vs. Prior Period**|How the current budget or forecast differs from the prior‑period results.|Helps assess whether the new plan is more aggressive or conservative relative to past performance.|

In practice, the analyst calculates the **numeric variance** (Actual – Plan or Actual – Prior) and then interprets it in terms of *cause* (price, volume, efficiency, mix, etc.) and *impact* (profitability, cash flow, strategic goals). The explanation typically follows a structured narrative:

1. **State the variance amount and direction** (e.g., “Revenue was $2 M above budget, a favourable variance of 5 %.”).  
2. **Identify the primary driver(s)** (e.g., “Higher sales volume in product A contributed $1.5 M, while a price increase added $0.5 M.”).  
3. **Assess significance** (e.g., “This variance exceeds the materiality threshold of 2 % and materially improves EBITDA.”).  
4. **Recommend actions** (e.g., “Maintain the pricing strategy and allocate additional marketing spend to sustain volume growth.”).
