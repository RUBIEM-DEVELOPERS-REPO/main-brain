## Answer
**Pre‑Meeting Brief Template – Executive‑Focused**

| Item | Content | Evidence |
|------|---------|----------|
| **Who** | Identify the executive (title, department, and any relevant background). Example: *Senior Marketing Executive, Jane Doe, who leads the brand‑strategy team and reports to the CMO.* | Definition of “executive” as “someone in a high position, especially in business, who makes decisions and puts them into action”【1】 |
| **What they want** | Summarise the executive’s objective for the meeting in one sentence. Example: *Jane wants approval for the Q3 digital‑media budget and alignment on the new product‑launch timeline.* | Executives are “a group of people who run a business or an organization” and are focused on decision‑making【1】 |
| **The ask** | State the concrete request the executive will make of the participants. Example: *Approve $1.2 M budget, provide market‑research data by Friday, and commit two senior designers to the launch team.* | Executives “make decisions and put them into action” – the ask is the decision they need from others【1】 |
| **Recommended answer** | Provide a concise, decision‑ready response for the executive, including any conditions or follow‑up actions. Example: *Approve $1.0 M now, with a $200 K contingency pending Q3 performance metrics; deliver research by Thursday; assign designers after the next sprint planning.* | Aligns with the executive’s role of “making decisions and putting them into action” and anticipates the next steps they will expect【1】 |

### How to Use the Brief
1. **Populate the table** before the meeting using the latest intel (e.g., recent reports, stakeholder input).  
2. **Distribute** to all attendees at least 24 hours in advance so they can prepare concise responses.  
3. **During the meeting**, keep the discussion focused on the four rows—this ensures the executive’s time is spent on decision‑making rather than background exposition.  

---  

**Key Takeaway:** An executive is a senior decision‑maker who expects clear, actionable requests and swift, decisive answers. Structuring pre‑meeting briefs around *who, what they want, the ask, and the recommended answer* aligns with that expectation and streamlines the meeting flow.
