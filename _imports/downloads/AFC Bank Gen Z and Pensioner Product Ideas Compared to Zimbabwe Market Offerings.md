AFC Bank Gen Z and Pensioner Product Ideas
Compared to Zimbabwe Market Offerings
Executive summary
AFC Bank (formerly Agribank) can credibly win  Gen Z (≈13–29)  and  pensioner  segments by combining
Zimbabwe-proven “baseline” offers  (low-cost/KYC-lite   accounts,   digital-first   access,   student   banking,
pensioner accounts, pension-backed lending) with a small set of high-differentiation “gap” products that
are not clearly offered—at least publicly—by Zimbabwean banks today.
On the Gen Z    side, the Zimbabwe market already has multiple KYC-lite/low-cost accounts (e.g., Stanbic’s
BluEase, POSB Youth Account, SuccessBank “Success Lite”), and there are strong student account offerings
(Ecobank Student Current, First Capital Ignition, BancABC Student Account, POSB LEVEL‑UP Student). 
However, the market is thinner on  explicit “freelancer/creator” cross-border payment products, and
especially thin on  digital agriculture workflow platforms  (farm management + financing + market
linkages) that go beyond lending. 
On the pensioner side, Zimbabwe already offers many senior/pensioner accounts with fee relief (CBZ,
FBC, POSB, NBS, ZB, CABS), and there is clear evidence of pension-linked credit (POSB NSSA Revolving
Loan Facility; FBC pension-based loans; NBS pension-backed personal loans).    The biggest “white
spaces”   are  health-dedicated   savings   +   discount   networks,    estate/legacy   planning   products
embedded in banking, and senior safety tooling (caregiver controls, simplified “safe mode” cards, and
formal assisted banking models). 
Implementation feasibility is high because AFC already advertises multi-currency personal accounts with
SMS alerts, and access via AFC Link channels (internet banking, mobile app, USSD *276#).  AFC’s
mobile app listing also indicates features like  open new account,   credit facilities application, and
biometric login, which lowers time-to-market for several proposals. 
Zimbabwe’s regulator and safety net context supports a pragmatic roll-out: RBZ publishes official lists of
operating   banking   institutions,   and   the   Deposit   Protection   Corporation   (DPC)   indicates   membership
includes all commercial banks,  building societies,  deposit-taking microfinance institutions,  POSB  , and
IDBZ   (among others).

Assumptions and methodology
Gen   Z   is   treated   as  ≈13–29  (with   distinct   sub-needs   for   minors/teens   vs   students   vs   first-jobbers).
Pensioners are treated as  retirees receiving pensions  (including NSSA beneficiaries and other pension
schemes), noting banks define “senior” differently (e.g., 60+, 65+, 75+). 
“Similar product exists” is assessed at a functional concept level, not by exact naming. A match is recorded
if   a   Zimbabwean   bank/building   society/deposit-taking   microfinance   institution   publicly   documents   a
product meeting most of the key use-case (e.g., “student account,” “pensioner account with fee relief,”
“pension-backed loan,” “ICT device financing”). Absence in this report means  no clearly documented
equivalent was found on official/public pages reviewed, not proof that no institution offers it privately or
via relationship channels. 
Primary sources were prioritized: bank websites and product pages, RBZ, NSSA, and DPC. 
Market reality check across Zimbabwe banks
Zimbabwe already demonstrates several segment patterns that AFC can copy fast (to become “table stakes”)
while choosing a few differentiators.
Low-cost, KYC-lite, and inclusion accounts are established. Stanbic offers BluEase, a low-cost account that
can be opened with a copy of an ID and is accessible via mobile app and USSD (247#).  POSB advertises a
Youth Account  (KYC-lite, ages 10–24).    Success Microfinance Bank offers  Success Lite* (“with just your ID...
maintenance cost free”) and explicitly ties eligibility to “access to loans based on your account activity,” a key
signal of transaction-led credit building. 
Student banking is explicit and competitive. First Capital’s Ignition is designed for students aged 18+ and
includes digital banking plus discounted monthly maintenance.  Ecobank Student Banking frames itself
as digital-first and “lower banking charges” when banking via e-channels.  BancABC’s Student Account
states “no monthly maintenance fees” and gives mobile/internet banking and a Visa debit card.  POSB’s
LEVEL‑UP Student Account is positioned specifically for tertiary students studying in Zimbabwe. 
Pensioner pricing relief is widely present. CBZ offers a Senior Citizens Account with no service charges and
“preferential treatment.”  FBC advertises that for seniors “over 60... our service is free,” emphasizing “no
queues.”  POSB has both Pensioner’s Account (65–74) and Senior Citizen Account (75+) explicitly at
low/no cost.  NBS offers a Seniors Account for pensioners above 60 with “zero monthly service fees” and
access to pension-backed personal loans.  ZB has a Heritage Account for senior citizens aged 65+. 
CABS highlights senior citizen discounts, stating transaction charges are not applied for clients 60+ (with
exceptions).  AFC itself publicly references “FREE Pensioner Accounts” in its business conditions (so a
branded “Golden Years” offer is a packaging + experience opportunity more than a new system build). 
Pension-linked lending is robust and tied into NSSA. POSB documents the NSSA Revolving Loan Facility for
qualifying NSSA pensioners receiving payouts through POSB.  FBC Microfinance documents pension-
based loans for government, ZESA, NRZ and NSSA pensioners.  NBS Seniors Account includes “pension-
backed personal loans.”  NSSA itself states that pensioners who bank with POSB and NBS “enjoy zero
transactions charges.”
