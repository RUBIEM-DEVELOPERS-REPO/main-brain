International Banking Products AFC Bank Can
Adapt for Gen Z and Pensioners in Zimbabwe
Executive summary
AFC Bank already has key “delivery rails” that make it realistic to adapt modern international banking
concepts quickly: AFC’s individual current/savings accounts are multi-currency (USD, ZWL, ZAR and other
tradable currencies),   SMS-alert enabled, and accessible through  internet banking, mobile app, and
USSD *276# (“AFC Link channels”).  AFC’s mobile app listing also indicates day-to-day capabilities such as
bill payments, transfers, statement download, beneficiaries, and biometric login, supporting digital-
first rollouts without rebuilding the whole stack. 
This report proposes 10 international-derived product proposals (5 Gen Z, 5 pensioner) that either (a)
have  no clearly documented Zimbabwe equivalent  based on publicly available product pages, or  (b)
strongly  complement AFC’s existing capabilities  (multi-currency + USSD/app channels + development/
agriculture identity). In Zimbabwe, regulators and safety-net framing matter; the  Deposit Protection
Corporation (DPC) states membership is mandatory for all licensed deposit-taking institutions regulated by
RBZ and includes commercial banks and POSB (among others), supporting trust messaging for new
segment products. 
From a demand lens, ZimStat’s  2023 Economic Census preliminary results indicate that a  majority of
establishments are informal  and   highlight   large   volumes   of   informal   micro-establishments,   which
supports   designing   Gen   Z   products   for   irregular-income   lifestyles   and   low-friction   digital   money
management.    For   pensioners,   NSSA   communications   emphasize   fee   sensitivity   and   benefits:
pensioners banking with POSB and NBS have been cited as enjoying zero transaction charges, and NSSA
has pointed to discounted groceries schemes—signals that “fee relief + savings + discounts + safety” value
propositions can resonate. 
Assumptions and methodology
Gen Z is assumed as ages ~13–29; pensioners are retirees receiving pensions (including NSSA and other
pension  arrangements).  “Not  currently  offered  in  Zimbabwe”  is  judged  conservatively  as:  no clearly
documented equivalent found on major Zimbabwean banks’ official product pages and public descriptions
reviewed,  not   a guarantee that no institution offers something similar privately. “Similar” is judged at a
functional level (core use-case and core features). 
International precedents are cited from official product pages (or official help/feature pages on the bank/
fintech’s primary domain where the product is implemented in-app).

AFC baseline and Zimbabwe context that shapes implementation
AFC’s baseline capability matters because several proposals are “feature layers” rather than entirely new
businesses:   AFC   already   provides   multi-currency   availability   and   access   through   app/USSD/internet
banking, plus transaction SMS alerts.  This means AFC can ship “experience products” (controls, pots,
alerts, access delegation) by combining  core banking configuration + channel UX + policy/process +
partnerships.
Zimbabwe’s structure supports focusing on:
-   Gen Z:  digital-first, low-friction tools for budgeting and safety, especially for irregular incomes in an
economy with a strong informal component. 
-   Pensioners:  fee relief, predictable access, discount ecosystems, and safer financial management with
trusted-family support. 
Product proposals for Gen Z
AFC Teen+ Parent Controls Account
Description (one paragraph)
A teen sub-account (ages ~13–17) that sits under a parent/guardian’s primary AFC profile, combining a teen
debit card + app/USSD access with guardian-set controls. Internationally, Revolut’s Kids & Teens product
shows a pattern where a parent/guardian onboards the child and the teen can transact and save toward
goals, with card controls available in-app. 
Key features
A teen “sub-wallet” under the guardian profile; guardian-set limits (ATM, online, contactless); merchant
category controls; allowance scheduling; “goals” pot; teen-to-teen transfers (optional, age-gated); real-time
spend notifications to guardian. 
Complementary services (partner types + value-add)
A school/college partner program for onboarding drives; payment acceptance partners for student spend
ecosystems; optional youth financial education content partners (NGOs/education platforms).
Implementation steps (with compliance notes)
1) Define age tiers and controls (13–15 vs 16–17) and map to AFC USSD/app menus.
2) Update onboarding/KYC policy: guardian full KYC + minor verification; capture parental consent and audit
logs (consumer protection + safeguarding).
3) Card program enhancements: introduce teen card BIN/rules with lower default limits; enable toggles for
ATM/online/contactless.
4) Build guardian controls UI in app; add USSD basics (balance, mini statement, allowance confirmation).
5) Add alerts: instant “teen spending” SMS/app push to guardian (AFC already supports SMS alerts). 
6) Pilot in 2–3 urban branches + 1 rural corridor; add a school/college onboarding week.
Complexity / time-to-market
Medium; ~2–4 months (faster if card processor supports granular controls).

Why it works in Zimbabwe
It gives parents in a largely informal and cash-heavy environment a safer on-ramp for teens to learn digital
money while maintaining controls—similar to global “parent-supervised independence” models. 
Customer benefits
Teens get independence, budgeting habits, and safer cashless spending; guardians reduce cash-handling
risk and can monitor/limit spend.
Bank benefits
Household acquisition (parent + teen); more card usage and payments volume; stronger deposit stickiness
via family relationship banking.
